ABOUT
THIS SERIES

In the weeks and months ahead, The Seattle Times’ “Hostile Waters” series will continue to explore and expose the plight of the southern resident killer whales, among the most-enduring symbols of our region and most-endangered animals. We’ll examine the role humans have played in their decline, what can be done about it and why it matters.

<%= t.renderMarkdown(archieml.story.section2) %>
Paul Spong founded OrcaLab in 1970. At this remote whale-research station on Hanson Island, B.C., Spong and his wife, Helena, and a cadre of volunteers document the daily lives — and spectacular sounds — of the northern resident orcas. Spong has created a network of remote cameras and underwater microphones to non-invasively monitor a 50-square mile swath of the orcas' core habitat. (Steve Ringman/The Seattle Times)

Pacific white-sided dolphins seem to fly as they skim the surface northeast of Vancouver Island. The environment of the northern residents is more like Puget Sound used to be, alive with wildlife and salmon and edged by forests cloaking the land to the waterline Forests nurture streams that support salmon that feed orcas in an unending cycle of life. Called the side-by-side tribe by the Namgis First Nation, northern resident orcas have the same close family bonds and culture as southern residents. Families, led by the matriarchs of the clan, stay together for life, and feed primarily on chinook salmon. (Steve Ringman/The Seattle Times)
<%= t.renderMarkdown(archieml.story.section5) %>
Northern residents enjoy a sanctuary created just for them in the Robson Bight (Michael Bigg) Ecological Reserve, where during most of the year no boat traffic is allowed. In addition to the cleaner, quieter water, the whales enjoy rubbing their bodies on the smooth stones of the beach seemingly just for the delight of it. The reserve includes the beach as well as an important salmon stream and the upland forest that protects it, also closed to all uses to preserve the sanctuary. (Courtesy of Explore.Org)

Killer whales, the oceans' top predator

Killer whales are the most widely distributed whales in the world and found in every ocean. Three orca ecotypes reside in the waters of the northern Pacific Coast. While their ranges overlap, they are not known to interact, and each ecotype is genetically distinct.

The southern residents draw a crowd anywhere they go. This ferry pauses in its route to let passengers enjoy orcas frolicking nearby. <%= t.renderMarkdown(archieml.story.section11) %>
Massive alteration of the Puget Sound ecosystem has destroyed the habitat that nourishes chinook salmon — the primary food for orca. Lack of food and industrial pollution, such as from the Superfund site in the Duwamish River, are hurting the orcas' ability to reproduce. Shipping noise also makes it harder for orcas to find food. Unlike the northern residents that bore 10 calves just last year, the orcas that frequent Puget Sound have not successfully reproduced in three years. (Steve Ringman/The Seattle Times)

The tiny dead calf born to J35, or Tahlequah, is seen in this photo alongside Tahlequah's head as she carries her through the water for a 16th straight day, in Swanson Channel, BC. Tahlequah clung to the calf, which lived only one half hour, for at least 17 days and more than 1,000 miles. Tahlequah's loss raised worldwide dismay at the plight of the critically endangered southern resident orca families. Mothers form close, lifelong bonds with their young. (Steve Ringman/The Seattle Times)
<%= t.renderMarkdown(archieml.story.section13) %>
The misshapen head of J50, a 3-year-old southern resident, is a sign of starvation. Hers was the third death in four months in a population that has not successfully reproduced in three years. Meanwhile the northern residents including this male, pictured at right, are growing in population and had 10 new calves in 2017. The two populations are the same type of orca, but live in very different environments. The southern residents thrived for thousands of years until their habitat was degraded by people. (Steve Ringman/The Seattle Times)

Despite an emergency rescue plan to save her, J50 became the third death in four months among the crticially endangered southern resident population of killer whales last summer. (Courtesy of Martin Haulena)
